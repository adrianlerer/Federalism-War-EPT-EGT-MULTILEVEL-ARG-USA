## 2.1 Introduction: Five Literatures, One Gap

This paper bridges five distinct literatures that have remained largely isolated despite addressing overlapping phenomena. We propose that clientelism—the strategic distribution of resources in exchange for political support—can only be understood by integrating insights from: (1) **public choice theory** on concentrated benefits and diffuse costs, (2) **fiscal federalism** on intergovernmental transfers and extraction, (3) **extended phenotype theory** from evolutionary biology, (4) **evolutionary game theory** on stable strategies, and (5) **signaling models** from institutional economics. Each literature offers partial insights, but none provides a unified framework capable of explaining why clientelism persists across regime types, state capacities, and development levels.

**The central gap**: Existing scholarship treats clientelism as either (a) a development stage that modernization will resolve (Lipset, 1959; Huntington, 1968), (b) a weak institutions problem that rule of law will cure (Acemoglu & Robinson, 2012), or (c) a rational response to information asymmetries that transparency will mitigate (Stokes, 2005). Our framework challenges all three assumptions by modeling clientelism as an **evolutionarily stable strategy** whose persistence depends not on development level or institutional quality, but on the **memetic fitness** of clientelistic practices relative to alternatives and the **regime type** in power.

This section proceeds as follows: We review public choice theory (2.2), fiscal federalism (2.3), extended phenotype theory (2.4), evolutionary game theory (2.5), and signaling models (2.6). Section 2.7 synthesizes these literatures and identifies the gap our framework addresses.

## 2.2 Public Choice Theory: Concentrated Benefits, Diffuse Costs

### 2.2.1 Foundational Contributions

**Buchanan and Tullock (1962)**: *The Calculus of Consent* established that democratic institutions systematically favor policies with concentrated benefits and diffuse costs. Rational voters remain "rationally ignorant" (Downs, 1957) about policies whose costs are spread thinly across the population, while beneficiaries organize to defend their gains. This asymmetry creates persistent inefficiencies.

**Key Insight for Clientelism**: Clientelistic transfers (e.g., *Manzaneras* payments, obras sociales subsidies) deliver **tangible, immediate benefits** to identifiable constituencies while imposing **small, delayed costs** on diffuse taxpayers. Public choice theory predicts such policies will dominate programmatic alternatives even when the latter generate higher aggregate welfare.

**Olson (1965)**: *The Logic of Collective Action* formalized the free-rider problem: large, diffuse groups (taxpayers, consumers) face prohibitive organization costs, while small, concentrated groups (unions, regional bosses) overcome collective action barriers. This explains why Argentine unions (300+ obras sociales controlling €10B+ annually) successfully resist reform despite representing <40% of the workforce.

**Application to Argentina**: Duhalde's Manzaneras program (1994–1999) exemplifies Olsonian logic:
- **Beneficiaries**: 1M+ women in 2,041 barrios → highly organized at neighborhood level
- **Costs**: Distributed across 12M+ Buenos Aires Province taxpayers → unorganized
- **Outcome**: Program survived Duhalde's exit (1999), De la Rúa crisis (2001), Kirchner expansion (2003–2015), and Macri reforms (2015–2019), only facing dismantling under Milei (2024)

### 2.2.2 Extensions: Dynamic Consistency and Ratchet Effects

**Tullock (1967)** and **Weingast et al. (1981)** identified **political ratchet effects**: once enacted, transfer programs create constituencies that fiercely resist retrenchment. Each beneficiary has strong incentives to defend "their" program, while dispersed taxpayers lack incentives to mobilize against any single program.

**Empirical Pattern (Argentina)**: Average lifespan of populist transfer programs is **62.4 years** (Lerer, 2025, SSRN), while liberal reforms average **7.1 years**—a survival ratio of 8.8:1. This asymmetry is inconsistent with median voter models (Meltzer & Richard, 1981), which predict policy volatility around the median preference. Instead, we observe **hysteresis**: clientelistic policies persist far beyond the governments that created them.

**Policy Implication**: Designing anti-clientelism strategies requires overcoming ratchet effects through **ideological reframing** (Milei's "gerentes de la pobreza") and **constituency substitution** (direct ANSES transfers create new beneficiaries independent of *punteros*).

### 2.2.3 Limitations for Studying Clientelism

Public choice theory explains **why** clientelistic policies emerge (concentrated benefits dominate) but not:
1. **Persistence mechanisms**: Why do clientelistic networks survive regime changes, crises, and reforms?
2. **Cross-national variation**: Why does Spain (OECD, high state capacity) exhibit Type B clientelism while Argentina exhibits Type A?
3. **Institutional diversity**: Why do some clientelistic systems use *punteros* (Type A), others direct subsidies (Type B), and others NGOs (USA Hybrid)?

We propose fiscal federalism literature addresses questions 2–3, while EPT addresses question 1.

## 2.3 Fiscal Federalism: Vertical Extraction and Horizontal Competition

### 2.3.1 Tiebout Competition and Its Limits

**Tiebout (1956)** proposed that subnational jurisdictions compete for mobile residents by offering efficient public good bundles. This "voting with feet" mechanism disciplines governments and prevents extraction. Empirical work (Oates, 1972, 1999) confirms Tiebout effects in some settings (US municipalities, Swiss cantons).

**The Clientelism Puzzle**: Tiebout competition predicts convergence toward efficient provision. Yet we observe:
- **Argentina**: Provinces with EI > 0.40 (La Matanza, Formosa) exhibit lower GDP growth but **higher population inflows** (via internal migration from efficient provinces)
- **Spain**: Catalonia and Andalusia (high transfer dependency) attract migrants despite high unemployment
- **USA**: California (EI +0.42) experiences net outmigration of middle class but net immigration of beneficiaries

**Resolution**: Tiebout assumes residents select jurisdictions based on **public good quality**. Our framework proposes beneficiaries select based on **transfer generosity**, creating **adverse selection** where extractive jurisdictions attract dependents while productive residents exit. This is consistent with Schram et al. (2009) finding that US welfare generosity attracts interstate migration.

### 2.3.2 The Soft Budget Constraint Problem

**Kornai (1979, 1986)**: Socialist firms faced "soft budget constraints"—expectation of bailouts led to inefficiency and moral hazard. Rodden (2006) and Wildasin (1997) extended this to subnational governments: if federal government credibly commits to bail out profligate regions, governors have incentives to over-borrow and over-spend.

**Application to Argentina**: Provinces with high EI systematically overspend, expecting federal bailouts:
- **Formosa** (EI +0.41): 78% of revenue from federal transfers; public employment 42% of workforce
- **La Matanza** (EI +0.57): Receives €1.2B+ annual federal transfers despite €800M local revenue

**Weingast's "Market-Preserving Federalism" (1995, 2009)**: Effective federalism requires:
1. **Hard budget constraints**: No federal bailouts
2. **Common market**: Free factor mobility
3. **Subnational autonomy**: Real policy discretion

**None hold in Argentina**: Federal government routinely bails out provinces (e.g., Fondo de Reparación Histórica), labor mobility is constrained by housing costs and clientelistic ties, and provinces have limited revenue autonomy (92% of taxes collected federally, redistributed via *Coparticipación*).

**Spain exhibits similar patterns**: Regional governments depend on central transfers (Catalonia demands €16B+ annual); €1.3B transport subsidy distributed pre-election.

### 2.3.3 The Race to the Bottom vs. Race to the Top

**Peterson and Rom (1990)** predicted interstate welfare competition would trigger a "race to the bottom"—states cut benefits to avoid becoming "welfare magnets." Empirical evidence is mixed (Brueckner, 2000).

**Our Finding**: We observe **race to the extraction frontier**—subnational units compete to **maximize federal transfers** rather than minimize local taxes. Evidence:
- **Argentina**: Provinces lobby for higher *Coparticipación* shares; Formosa governor Gildo Insfrán (1995–present) built career on securing federal funds
- **Spain**: Autonomous communities compete for Madrid subsidies; Catalan independence movement partly motivated by transfer resentment
- **USA**: States compete for federal Medicaid expansion, refugee resettlement funds

**Implication**: Fiscal federalism alone cannot explain why **some** subnational units choose cooperation (Catamarca EI −0.08, Misiones EI −0.09) while others choose extraction. We propose EPT provides the missing mechanism.

## 2.4 Extended Phenotype Theory: Genes, Memes, and Institutions

### 2.4.1 Dawkins's Original Formulation

**Dawkins (1982)**: Genes don't just build bodies—they modify environments to enhance their own reproduction. A beaver's dam is a "gene's eye view" of evolution: the dam persists across beaver generations, creating conditions where beaver genes outcompete otter genes.

**Key Concepts**:
1. **Environmental Modification**: Phenotypes extend beyond organism boundaries
2. **Reproductive Enhancement**: Modifications increase gene frequency in future generations
3. **Persistence**: Structures outlive individual organisms
4. **Heritability**: Effects transmit across generations

**Application to Politics (Lerer, 2025, SSRN)**: Populist policies (e.g., Aguinaldo, obras sociales) function as extended phenotypes:
- **Modify environment**: Create dependency structures (52% of Argentines receive direct state transfers)
- **Enhance reproduction**: Make populist parties more electable in future cycles
- **Persist**: Aguinaldo survived 80 years, 22 governments, 6 coups
- **Heritable**: Children born into union healthcare system normalize corporatist provision

### 2.4.2 Memetics: Cultural Replicators

**Dawkins (1976)** introduced "memes"—ideas that spread like genes, subject to selection pressures. Subsequent work (Boyd & Richerson, 1985; Sperber, 1996; Henrich, 2016) formalized cultural evolution theory.

**Memetic Fitness Function (Lerer, 2025)**:
$$F(M) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} v_i^{w_i} \times R(M) \times C(M,E) \times N(M)$$

Where populist memes exhibit **216:1 fitness advantage** over liberal memes in Argentine ecosystem due to:
- **Cognitive Simplicity**: Binary narratives ("us vs. them") vs. complex market equilibria
- **Gratification Speed**: 2–4 weeks (subsidy check) vs. 3–5 years (growth effects)
- **Tangibility**: Visible benefits (aguinaldo) vs. counterfactual (avoided crisis)
- **Emotional Activation**: Anger, hope, belonging vs. weak satisfaction
- **Defense Mechanisms**: "Empire attacks" vs. academic arguments

**Extension to Clientelism**: We propose **clientelistic institutions** exhibit even higher memetic fitness than generic populism because they:
1. Deliver **personalized** benefits (*puntero* knows your name) vs. universal programs
2. Create **reciprocity norms** (you helped me, I vote for you) vs. anonymous transfers
3. Embed in **social networks** (family, neighborhood) vs. bureaucratic systems

### 2.4.3 Niche Construction Theory

**Odling-Smee et al. (2003)**: Organisms don't just adapt to environments—they modify environments to suit themselves, creating feedback loops. Beavers build dams → create ponds → favor aquatic vegetation → favor beavers. This is "niche construction."

**Application to Clientelism**:
- **Type A (Punteros)**: Construct territorial niches (villas, asentamientos) where state services are absent, making *puntero* intermediation essential. As villas grow (+82% in Buenos Aires 1990–2015), *puntero* power grows.
- **Type B (Direct Subsidies)**: Construct dependency niches where citizens expect universal transfers (Spain €60 transport pass → 2M beneficiaries → electoral base defending subsidy).
- **Hybrid (NGOs)**: Construct legal niches (sanctuary cities) where federal law is selectively enforced, making NGO intermediation necessary for accessing services.

**Key Insight**: Clientelistic institutions don't just **respond** to poverty/inequality—they **construct niches** where clientelism becomes more fitness-enhancing than alternatives.

## 2.5 Evolutionary Game Theory: Stable Strategies and Invasion Resistance

### 2.5.1 Maynard Smith's ESS Concept

**Maynard Smith (1982)**: An Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS) is a strategy that, if adopted by a population, cannot be invaded by any alternative strategy. Formally:

A strategy $S$ is an ESS if for all alternative strategies $S'$:
1. $E(S, S) \geq E(S', S)$ [Nash equilibrium]
2. If $E(S', S) = E(S, S)$, then $E(S, S') > E(S', S')$ [Stability]

Where $E(A, B)$ is the expected payoff of strategy $A$ against strategy $B$.

**Application to Clientelism**: We model political competition as an evolutionary game:

| | Voters Support Clientelism | Voters Support Programmatic |
|---|---|---|
| **Politicians Offer Clientelism** | (3, 3) | (4, 1) |
| **Politicians Offer Programmatic** | (1, 2) | (2†, 4†) |

†Payoffs realized only if time horizon exceeds one electoral cycle (8–10 years)

**Proposition (ESS Conditions)**: Clientelism is an ESS if:
- Immediate payoffs (3) exceed delayed payoffs (2) for typical voter discount rates
- Politicians face short time horizons (next election, not long-run growth)

**Evidence (Argentina)**: No liberal government since 1946 has completed a full reproductive cycle (two consecutive terms with policy continuity). Average liberal government duration: 3.2 years.

### 2.5.2 Replicator Dynamics and Path Dependence

**Weibull (1995)**, **Nowak (2006)**: Strategies reproduce proportionally to their payoff advantage. If $x(t)$ is the frequency of clientelism at time $t$:

$$\frac{dx}{dt} = x(t)[1-x(t)][E(\text{Client}, \text{mixed}) - E(\text{Program}, \text{mixed})]$$

**Key Implication**: Even if programmatic policies are **slightly** more efficient (higher long-run payoff), clientelism dominates if it has **any** short-run payoff advantage. The basin of attraction around the clientelistic equilibrium is large.

**Historical Pattern**: Argentina spent 70% of years since 1946 under clientelistic regimes (Peronism, Kirchnerism), 20% under military dictatorships (which often continued clientelistic structures), and only 10% under liberal experiments (Martínez de Hoz, Menem late-term, Macri)—all of which were reversed.

### 2.5.3 Conditional ESS Stability (Our Contribution)

**Standard ESS theory assumes fixed payoffs.** We propose ESS stability is **conditional on regime type**:

- **Establishment Regimes** (Peronism, PSOE, US Democrats): Clientelism is ESS because ruling coalition **benefits** from extraction
- **Counter-Establishment Regimes** (Milei, Trump, Orbán): Clientelism ESS **may be disrupted** because:
  1. **Ideological reframing** changes payoffs (gerentes de la pobreza → voters support dismantling)
  2. **Constituency substitution** creates alternative stable state (direct transfers → new beneficiaries)
  3. **Rapid poverty reduction** validates anti-clientelism signal

**Early Evidence (Milei, 18 months)**: Consistent with conditional ESS but insufficient for validation (requires 8–10 years).

## 2.6 Signaling Models: Fryer's Non-Violent Power Construction

### 2.6.1 Fryer (2024): Establishment vs. Counter-Establishment Dynamics

**Fryer (2024)** models how Counter-Establishment leaders signal commitment by constructing **non-violent power bases** that credibly threaten Establishment interests without triggering repression.

**Key Mechanisms**:
1. **Signaling via Costly Actions**: Actions that benefit Counter-Establishment but harm Establishment signal commitment (e.g., Milei's chainsaw theatrics, Trump's wall)
2. **Base Construction**: Creating constituencies whose welfare depends on Counter-Establishment success (e.g., farmers benefiting from deregulation)
3. **Coalition Impossibility**: Making cooperation with Establishment costly (Milei's "casta" framing makes PRO alliance fragile)

**Application to Anti-Clientelism**:
- **Milei's "Gerentes de la Pobreza" Framing**: Redefines *punteros* from "community leaders helping the poor" to "poverty managers extracting rents"
- **Dismantling 300+ Units**: Costly signal (short-term poverty risk, electoral backlash) that credibly commits to Type B system
- **Direct ANSES Transfers**: Creates new constituency (beneficiaries who bypass *punteros*) whose interests align with Milei

### 2.6.2 Acemoglu & Robinson: Commitment Problems

**Acemoglu & Robinson (2006)**: Political transitions fail when elites cannot credibly commit to not expropriating winners. Clientelistic systems have similar commitment problems:
- **Establishment → Counter-Establishment**: How does Milei credibly commit to not becoming a new *puntero* himself? (Solution: Digitalization creates transparent, algorithmic transfers)
- **Counter-Establishment → Establishment**: How does next government credibly commit to not restoring *punteros*? (No solution yet—requires ESS shift)

**Our Contribution**: We propose Fryer's signaling model explains **disruption** of clientelistic ESS, but ESS theory explains **reversion risk**. Milei's reforms may prove unstable if successor government (2027+) restores old equilibrium.

## 2.7 Synthesis: The Unified Framework

### 2.7.1 What Each Literature Contributes

| Literature | Contribution to Clientelism | Limitation |
|-----------|------------------------------|------------|
| **Public Choice** | Why clientelism emerges (concentrated benefits) | Doesn't explain persistence or variation |
| **Fiscal Federalism** | How federal transfers enable extraction | Doesn't explain why some units cooperate |
| **Extended Phenotype** | Mechanism of persistence (environmental modification) | Lacks formal game theory |
| **Evolutionary Game Theory** | Conditions for stability (ESS) | Assumes fixed strategies, not institutional evolution |
| **Signaling Models** | How Counter-Establishment disrupts | Doesn't model long-term stability |

### 2.7.2 Our Five-Layer Integration

We propose clientelism is best understood as a **five-layer emergent phenomenon**:

**Layer 1 (Tocqueville)**: Subnational despotism as structural possibility  
**Layer 2 (Public Choice)**: Concentrated benefits create selection pressure favoring clientelism  
**Layer 3 (EPT)**: Clientelistic institutions modify environment to self-replicate  
**Layer 4 (EGT)**: Clientelism constitutes conditional ESS  
**Layer 5 (Fryer)**: Counter-Establishment signaling can disrupt ESS (tentatively)  

**Integration Example (Argentina)**:
1. Federal transfers create opportunity (Tocqueville)
2. *Manzaneras* deliver concentrated benefits (Public Choice)
3. Program creates 1M+ beneficiaries who defend it (EPT niche construction)
4. Populist parties win elections by maintaining program (ESS)
5. Milei disrupts by reframing + digitalization (Fryer signaling)
6. **Open question**: Will disruption persist or revert? (ESS conditional stability)

### 2.7.3 The Gap in Existing Literature

**No existing framework explains all five empirical patterns**:

1. **Cross-regime persistence**: Why clientelism survives dictatorship → democracy → dictatorship → democracy (Argentina 1946–1983–2024)
2. **OECD presence**: Why Spain (high state capacity) exhibits Type B clientelism
3. **Institutional diversity**: Why Argentina uses *punteros*, Spain uses direct subsidies, USA uses NGOs
4. **Asymmetric survival**: Why clientelistic policies last 62.4 years vs. liberal policies 7.1 years
5. **Conditional disruption**: Why Milei (maybe) succeeds where Macri, De la Rúa, and Menem failed

**Our framework addresses all five**:
1. EPT explains cross-regime persistence (extended phenotypes outlive creators)
2. Public Choice + EI explain OECD (high state capacity enables Type B extraction)
3. Fiscal Federalism + EPT phenotypes explain diversity (different institutional niches)
4. ESS + Memetic Fitness explain asymmetric survival (clientelism is stable attractor)
5. Fryer + Conditional ESS explain disruption (ideological distance + signaling)

## 2.8 Related Empirical Literature

### 2.8.1 Clientelism in Latin America

**Stokes (2005)**, **Levitsky & Roberts (2011)**: Latin American clientelism persists because:
- **Information asymmetries**: Politicians need brokers to monitor vote delivery
- **Weak parties**: Programmatic politics requires strong party organizations
- **Inequality**: High Gini creates demand for redistribution

**Our Challenge**: These factors don't explain:
- Spain (low inequality, strong parties, high transparency) exhibits Type B clientelism
- Catamarca/Misiones (Argentina, high inequality, weak parties) exhibit cooperation (EI < 0)
- Milei (weak party, high inequality) appears to dismantle clientelism

**Our Explanation**: Clientelism is not a function of structural conditions alone—it's an **ESS conditional on regime type**.

### 2.8.2 Distributive Politics

**Cox & McCubbins (1986)**: Parties target **core supporters** (reinforce) vs. **swing voters** (persuade).  
**Diaz-Cayeros et al. (2016)**: Mexican PRI targeted **both** cores (insurance) and swings (mobilization).

**Our Finding**: Clientelistic regimes target **dependents** (not cores or swings):
- **Argentina**: Manzaneras target villa residents (30% turnout, 85% Peronist)
- **Spain**: €60 transport pass targets urban commuters (high density, swing regions)
- **USA**: Sanctuary cities target undocumented immigrants (non-voters but family/community influencers)

**Implication**: Standard distributive politics models (median voter, swing voter) fail to predict clientelistic targeting. Our EI framework better predicts: target jurisdictions where **extraction efficiency is highest** (Census + Fiscal + Social dimensions).

### 2.8.3 Federalism and Redistribution

**Rodden (2006)**: Left parties prefer centralization (uniform redistribution); Right parties prefer decentralization (Tiebout sorting).

**Our Complication**: Clientelistic parties prefer **decentralization with soft budget constraints**:
- Peronism (left) decentralizes via provinces (Formosa, La Matanza autonomy)
- PSOE (left) decentralizes via autonomous communities (Catalonia, Andalusia)
- US Democrats (left) decentralize via sanctuary cities (California, Minnesota)

**Why?** Decentralization enables **extraction** while centralization enables **monitoring**. Clientelistic regimes prefer the former; programmatic regimes prefer the latter.

## 2.9 Conclusion: Toward a Unified Framework

The literatures reviewed above provide essential components but remain isolated. Public choice explains **why** clientelism is attractive (concentrated benefits); fiscal federalism explains **how** intergovernmental transfers enable extraction; EPT explains **persistence mechanisms** (environmental modification); EGT explains **stability conditions** (ESS); and signaling models explain **disruption pathways** (Fryer's non-violent base construction).

**Our contribution**: We synthesize these five literatures into a unified framework centered on:
1. **Six-dimensional Extraction Index (EI)**: Quantifies clientelism across countries/regimes
2. **Conditional ESS**: Stability depends on regime type (Establishment vs. Counter-Establishment)
3. **Three institutional types**: Type A (puntero), Type B (direct subsidy), Hybrid (NGO)
4. **Transborder extraction**: Novel sixth phenotype (Spain rail credits to Morocco)
